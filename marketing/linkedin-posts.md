## Post 1 — Build breakdown (Multihull Central)

A yacht can cost more than a house. Its website can't feel like a classified ad.

When I built the e-commerce platform for Multihull Central — a high-end yacht brokerage — the hard part wasn't making it look premium. It was the data.

Every listing carried dozens of specs: length, cabins, year, engine hours, price across currencies. Buyers needed to filter and compare all of it without feeling overwhelmed.

So I built a custom WordPress theme with ACF, modeled every yacht as clean, structured data, and made the filtering feel instant.

The lesson I keep relearning: on premium sites, "looks good" is the baseline. What actually sells is making a complicated decision feel simple.

That's the gap between a brochure and a tool that closes.

## Post 2 — Value / contrarian take

I've shipped 17+ websites and online stores. The ones that make money have almost nothing to do with how "pretty" they are.

Here's what actually sets them apart:

1/ One clear job. The best sites do ONE thing well — book the call, sell the product, capture the lead. Not "be everything to everyone."

2/ Speed. A site that takes 5 seconds to load doesn't just annoy people — it kills the sale before your offer is even on screen.

3/ An obvious next step. If a visitor thinks "okay… now what?", you've already lost them.

4/ Built to last. A site that breaks on every plugin update isn't an asset. It's a liability with your logo on it.

Most small businesses don't have a design problem. They have a "this site isn't doing its job" problem.

Fix the job first. Pretty is easy after that.

Which one does your site get wrong? 👇
